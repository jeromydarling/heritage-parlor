About Heritage Parlor

A curated collection of parlor amusements drawn from thirteen public domain books published between 1857 and 1917. Every entry is free to use, share, and adapt.

Source Books

  • 1857
    The Magician's Own Book — Anonymous. Comprehensive conjuring manual with tricks, puzzles, and scientific recreations.
  • 1858
    The Sociable — George Arnold & Frank Cahill. 1,001 home amusements with 500 engravings.
  • 1869
    Every Boy's Book — Edmund Routledge. A massive encyclopedia with 600+ illustrations.
  • 1876
    Modern Magic — Professor Hoffmann. The foundational Victorian conjuring manual.
  • 1881
    Cassell's Book of In-door Amusements — The definitive Victorian household games compendium.
  • 1893
    Puzzles Old and New — Professor Hoffmann. 400+ mechanical, mathematical, and word puzzles.
  • 1894
    Traditional Games of England, Scotland, and Ireland, Vol. I — Alice Bertha Gomme. Scholarly collection of British folk games.
  • 1897
    The Game of Logic — Lewis Carroll. A board game for teaching syllogistic reasoning.
  • 1898
    Traditional Games, Vol. II — Alice Bertha Gomme. Continuation of the folk games collection.
  • 1907
    The Canterbury Puzzles — Henry Dudeney. Chaucer-inspired mathematical brain-teasers.
  • 1914
    Foster's Complete Hoyle — R. F. Foster. Rules for every indoor game of the era.
  • 1916
    My Book of Indoor Games — Clarence Squareman. Entertaining games for the whole family.
  • 1917
    Amusements in Mathematics — Henry Dudeney. 430 original recreational mathematics puzzles.

A Living Tradition

Heritage Parlor Game Nights

Revive the Victorian parlour — an evening of laughter, wit, and genuine togetherness. No screens required.

✦

What Is a Heritage Parlor Game Night?

A Heritage Parlor Game Night is a dedicated evening — two to three hours — built entirely around the games in this collection. The Victorians were masters of sociable entertainment: games that required nothing more than sharp wit, willing players, and perhaps a deck of cards. A Game Night brings that spirit back.

There are no devices, no downloads, and no winner-takes-all prizes. The point is the play itself — the groans at a bad riddle, the laughter when a charade goes wrong, the quiet satisfaction of a puzzle finally solved.

How to Host One

I
Pick 5–7 games. Browse the collection and select a balanced mix — one icebreaker, a couple of group games, and one or two that reward cleverness. Aim for variety in style: acting, guessing, wordplay, and perhaps a quiet puzzle for intermission.
II
Print the instructions. Every game in this collection has a print button. Print one copy per table so players can follow along. The original Victorian language is half the fun.
III
Gather your props. Most games need nothing at all. For others: a deck of cards, slips of paper, pencils, or a blindfold. Check the "What You Need" field on each game card.
IV
Appoint a Master of Ceremonies. One person reads the rules aloud at the start of each game and keeps the evening moving. Rotate the role through the night.
V
Set the atmosphere. Dim the overhead lights. Put on something instrumental and low. If it's winter, light a candle. The Victorians understood that atmosphere is half of hospitality.

Suggested Game Night Menus

A themed evening gives the night a satisfying shape. Each menu is built around games already in this collection — filter by category to find them.

🃏

Card Shark Night

An evening of clever card play drawn from Foster's Complete Hoyle and Cassell's. Perfect for 4–8 players who enjoy a competitive edge.

  • Open with a quick card trick to set the mood
  • Two rounds of a Victorian trick-taking game
  • A card-based memory or prediction game
  • Close with a solitaire puzzle challenge — fastest solver wins
Filter: Card Games
✨

Magic Show Night

Each guest learns one trick from Professor Hoffmann's Modern Magic and performs it for the group. Beginners welcome — the deceptions are Victorian, not Vegas.

  • Assign tricks in advance so everyone can practice
  • Brief intermission for guests to "rehearse" in private
  • Each performer gives a dramatic Victorian-style introduction
  • Audience votes on "Most Convincing Illusion"
Filter: Magic Tricks
📝

Word Wizard Night

For the wordsmiths in the room. A succession of Victorian word games that reward vocabulary, lateral thinking, and a quick tongue. Ideal for 4–12 players.

  • Start with a quick-fire rhyming game to warm up
  • A round of acting proverbs for physical comedy
  • A longer riddle or logic puzzle for quiet contemplation
  • Close with a communal story game — each player adds one sentence
Filter: Word Games, Parlor Games
🧩

Puzzle Parlour Night

Drawn from Dudeney and Professor Hoffmann — mechanical puzzles, mathematical brain-teasers, and optical illusions. More contemplative; works well in pairs.

  • Print three or four Dudeney puzzles per table
  • Work in pairs — first pair to solve all wins the round
  • Finish with a group puzzle that requires collective reasoning
  • Read aloud the Victorian solution commentary for laughs
Filter: Puzzles
🌿

Folk & Field Night

Games from Alice Bertha Gomme's Traditional Games of England, Scotland, and Ireland. Best outdoors or in a large space. A hit with mixed ages.

  • One singing game to open — everyone participates
  • A chasing or tagging game for energy
  • A quieter forfeits game to close the evening
  • Read Gomme's scholarly notes — the folklore is fascinating
Filter: Folk Games, Physical Games
🔬

Scientific Wonder Night

Optical illusions, scientific tricks, and natural philosophy demonstrations from The Magician's Own Book. Best for curious minds of all ages.

  • Begin with a visual illusion — discuss why it fools the eye
  • A physics-based trick that seems impossible
  • A chemistry or electricity demonstration (review safety notes!)
  • End with a discussion: which tricks would have shocked a Victorian?
Filter: Science

Tips for Different Group Sizes

2–3
Intimate

Lean on puzzles, card games, and two-player strategy. The quiet games shine here — the wit is more visible in a small group. Dudeney puzzles work especially well as a cooperative activity.

4–8
A Parlour Party

The sweet spot for most Victorian parlour games. Acting games, word games, and trick-taking card games all shine. Split into two teams of four for competitive rounds.

9–20
A Full Evening

Run games in parallel across two or three tables. Rotate between tables mid-evening. Close with a whole-group game — charades or acting proverbs work beautifully at this scale.

20+
Community Event

Stations work best at this size. Set up four or five game tables with printed instructions and a host at each. A central MC announces rounds and moves people between tables every 20 minutes.
